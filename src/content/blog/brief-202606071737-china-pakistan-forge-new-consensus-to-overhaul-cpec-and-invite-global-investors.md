<p>Pakistani Prime Minister Shehbaz Sharif and Chinese President Xi Jinping agreed on Tuesday to overhaul the China-Pakistan Economic Corridor, pledging to turn the Gwadar port into a regional hub and opening the flagship Belt and Road project to third-party investors. The “new broad consensus”, announced after Sharif met Xi and Premier Li Qiang in Beijing, prioritises upgrading the Khunjerab Pass and the Karakoram Highway — the main overland link between the two neighbours.</p>
<p>The revamped corridor aims to deepen trade connectivity across South and Central Asia. Islamabad, struggling with an economic crisis while mediating a temporary US-Iran ceasefire and managing border tensions with Afghanistan, hopes the move will unlock much-needed foreign capital.</p>
<p>In a significant concession to Beijing, Pakistan committed to “targeted steps” to bolster security for Chinese nationals and projects, directly addressing Beijing’s primary concern after repeated militant attacks on its workers. The joint statement underscored a shared determination to safeguard personnel and investments.</p>
<p>On regional diplomacy, China praised Pakistan’s role in facilitating Iran ceasefire talks, and both sides renewed support for a five-point peace initiative in West Asia. Pakistan reiterated its adherence to the one-China principle, calling Taiwan an “inalienable” part of China and opposing any form of independence. The two capitals also condemned the use of Afghan territory by militant groups such as the Tehreek-e-Taliban Pakistan for cross-border attacks, while Beijing welcomed Islamabad’s dialogue efforts with Afghanistan.</p>
<p>For the Global South, the CPEC overhaul signals a potential opening for broader participation in Chinese-led infrastructure projects. Yet persistent security risks in Pakistan’s restive provinces are likely to test the corridor’s long-term viability, even as both sides prepare to invite new partners to the joint venture.</p>
