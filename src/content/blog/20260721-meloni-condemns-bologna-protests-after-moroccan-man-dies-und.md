Italian Prime Minister Giorgia Meloni has condemned overnight protests in Bologna that left dozens of police officers injured, following the death of a Moroccan man while in police custody.

Speaking on Tuesday, Meloni pledged a fair investigation into the death of 42-year-old Abderrahim Fakir, but accused demonstrators of seeking 'confrontation' rather than 'truth', according to Al Jazeera.

Fakir died on Sunday after police responded to reports of a man behaving aggressively and damaging a vehicle. Footage widely shared online shows him face down while two officers restrain him, calling for help and saying he cannot breathe before falling still.

Protests broke out Sunday night and resumed Monday evening in central Bologna, where demonstrators hurled firecrackers, bottles and chairs at police, who responded with tear gas and water cannons. The Italian Coordination of Police Officers reported 56 officers injured.

'Those who chose to use this incident as a pretext to lay waste to the city, attack officers and spread violence … were not seeking the truth: they were seeking confrontation,' Meloni declared.

She added that 'any responsibilities' must 'be ascertained with the utmost rigour' and that the truth must be pursued 'without prejudice and without leniency toward anyone.'

The footage has drawn comparisons to the 2020 killing of George Floyd by US police in Minneapolis. Prosecutors have opened an investigation, reigniting debate in Italy over the use of force during arrests.

Interior Minister Matteo Piantedosi said he had 'full confidence' the inquiry would establish the facts, telling daily Il Messaggero that officers deserve the same 'presumption of innocence' as any other citizen.

The case has drawn reactions across the political spectrum. Members of the populist right-wing governing coalition have defended the officers involved, while opposition politicians criticized the government's handling and claimed its policies promote violence.

Centre-left legislator Francesco Boccia warned against Italy adopting an 'ICE model' of policing, referring to the US Immigration and Customs Enforcement agency, which has been involved in several killings of protesters.

Lawyer Barbara Spinelli, who met Fakir last year when he applied to renew his residency permit, told ANSA news agency that he was 'a very kind, well-educated, respectful person' who had worried about being expelled from Italy despite living there legally.

The incident highlights ongoing tensions in Italy over policing and migration policies, with the Global South facing disproportionate scrutiny in European law enforcement.
