<p>ISLAMABAD — Pakistan on Thursday accused India of “weaponising water” following New Delhi’s suspension of the Indus Waters Treaty, escalating a dispute that threatens the water security of 250 million Pakistanis and risks aggravating tensions between the nuclear-armed neighbours.</p>
<p>Pakistan’s Foreign Ministry spokesperson Tahir Andrabi told reporters that two Indian projects on the Chenab River would undermine the foundational 1960 pact governing cross-border waterways. Andrabi said New Delhi had not consulted Islamabad on either initiative. “These projects confirm that India seems to weaponise water,” he said, warning of “dangerous implications” for Pakistan’s economy and regional stability.</p>
<p>India suspended the bilateral treaty after a deadly April 2025 attack on tourists in Indian-administered Kashmir, which New Delhi blamed on Pakistan-backed militants — a charge Islamabad denies. The move preceded intense drone, missile and artillery exchanges the following month that killed nearly 70 people on both sides.</p>
<p>The disputed projects include a National Hydroelectric Power Corporation tunnel scheme to divert Chenab water to the Beas basin, tendered in May, and sediment removal at the Salal Power Station announced in January. India’s Foreign Ministry maintains it is within its rights to proceed. It recently rejected a May 15 ruling by the Hague-based Court of Arbitration — which Islamabad says affirmed the treaty’s continued validity — dismissing the body as “illegally constituted” and insisting the suspension “remains in force.”</p>
<p>Andrabi said any measure endangering Pakistan’s water, food or economic security was “unacceptable” and that Islamabad would “retain all options necessary” to safeguard its rights. Pakistan has previously warned it would view attempts to alter cross-border water flows as an “act of war.”</p>
<p>The Indus Waters Treaty had long provided a rare diplomatic channel between the rivals, surviving three armed conflicts over more than six decades. Analysts warn that water is emerging as a flashpoint as climate change and population growth strain agricultural sectors critical to both economies.</p>
