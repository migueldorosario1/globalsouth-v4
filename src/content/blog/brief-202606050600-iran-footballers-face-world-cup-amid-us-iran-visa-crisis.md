<p>Iran footballers Saeid Ezatolahi and Mohammad Ghorbani have detailed how the country’s war with the United States and Israel is upending their World Cup preparations, as visa barriers and a forced relocation of their training base add an extraordinary layer of political tension to the tournament.</p>
<p>The conflict, which erupted on February 28 and has been locked in an uneasy ceasefire since April 8, prompted US visa processing delays that compelled Iran to abandon its original camp in Tucson, Arizona, and shift to Tijuana, Mexico. The squad spent more than two weeks training in Turkey and received Mexican entry permits this week, but players remain unable to enter the sole host nation where all their Group G matches are scheduled.</p>
<p>Ezatolahi, a 29-year-old midfielder heading to his third World Cup, told the Associated Press the political backdrop weighs heavily on the team. “At the same time, we are following the news in our country and the political things, of course, can affect the mind of the players,” he said. He acknowledged the added pressure of performing before the large Iranian community in the Los Angeles area, where Iran will face New Zealand on June 15 and Belgium six days later. “I just wish we can make them proud and show them that Iranians are prepared for every hard job in the world.”</p>
<p>Ghorbani, 24, preparing for his first World Cup, said the squad’s mission was to deliver solace to compatriots battered by war. “We know that our people have been going through a lot of difficulties throughout the war, and we are going there for them, to get the best results for their joy,” he said in Farsi.</p>
<p>With no permanent peace in sight and flare-ups continuing in the region, Iran’s players insist the shared ambition of unifying a nation under one flag can transcend the hostilities. The side is not required to enter the US until June 14, one day before its opener, and finishes group play in Seattle against Egypt on June 26.</p>
